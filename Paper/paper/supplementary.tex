\section{Results at the 99th-percentile threshold}
\label{sec:supp99}

The main paper analyses the $95$th-percentile index, which has $417$ extreme days
($287$ train, $130$ test). The $99$th-percentile index is far scarcer --- only
$137$ extreme days in thirty years ($101$ train, $36$ test) --- which precludes
the held-out intensity analysis (that requires extreme days in both train and
test across several splits). For completeness we report here the results of the
full model set at the $99$th percentile, including the GP--EVT hurdle. The
pattern of the main paper repeats: the GP--EVT hurdle is again the best model,
while the more flexible response distributions (notably the zero-inflated
log-normal) fall well below climatology.

Table~\ref{tab:supp99} shows the bake-off (seed-averaged over three seeds, horizon
$h=1$), scored against climatology as in the main paper. The qualitative picture
matches the $95$th percentile but is weaker and noisier, as expected with a third
as many events:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Occurrence remains weakly predictable.} A plain calibrated logistic
regression with the ERA5 drivers beats climatology (Brier skill score
$+0.015$), improving on index history alone ($+0.001$); the gain again comes
chiefly from the convective-instability and circulation fields. Resolution
(informativeness) is positive but smaller than at the $95$th percentile
($0.36\times10^{-3}$ versus $0.83\times10^{-3}$).
\item \textbf{The distributional models fall below climatology.} With only $101$
training exceedances, the zero-inflated gamma and eGPD regressions are slightly
worse than climatology on the Brier score, and the zero-inflated log-normal is
again catastrophically miscalibrated --- echoing, more sharply, the main-paper
finding that the response distribution matters and that data scarcity penalises
the more flexible models.
\item \textbf{The GP--EVT hurdle wins again.} The GP--EVT
hurdle is the best model at the $99$th percentile too --- the highest Brier skill
($+0.027$, above logistic's $+0.015$) and the \emph{only} model with positive
threshold-weighted CRPS skill ($+0.016$) --- echoing its main-paper win, though
the margin is slim at this data volume.
\item \textbf{The tail is all but climatology-bound.} Apart from the GP
hurdle's slim positive margin, no model beats the climatological tail on the
threshold-weighted CRPS (all other skill scores $\le 0$), and the scarce
exceedances do not support the held-out intensity test.
\end{itemize}

In short, the $99$th-percentile threshold tells the same story as the $95$th ---
weak but genuine occurrence predictability led by convective instability, a tail
only the GP--EVT hurdle edges past, and a penalty on over-flexible response
distributions (the zero-inflated log-normal) --- but
with too few events to support the intensity analysis or to add to the main
conclusions.

\section{Paris: per-city diagnostic figures}
\label{sec:supp-paris}

The main paper shows the data, calibration, and horizon-decay diagnostics
(Figures~1, 2, and~4 there) for London as the representative city, while the
core comparative results --- the bake-off, the held-out intensity analysis, and
the unified hurdle --- are reported for both cities. For completeness we give the
Paris counterparts of the three single-city diagnostic figures here. They are
qualitatively identical to London: a zero-inflated, right-skewed, short-memory
index with a summer convective peak (Figure~\ref{fig:data-paris}); well-calibrated
exceedance probabilities from the simple logistic model and the GP--EVT
hurdle, with the zero-inflated log-normal systematically overconfident
(Figure~\ref{fig:reliability-paris});
and occurrence skill decaying toward the prevalence floor with lead time while
conformal coverage stays at the nominal level (Figure~\ref{fig:horizon-paris}).
Paris differs from London only in having a higher extreme-day prevalence (a more
convective regime), consistent with its stronger predictability in the main text.

\section{Feature importance and horizon-wise occurrence skill}
\label{sec:supp-features}

The main paper reports, in prose, that antecedent convective-instability and
circulation indices dominate the occurrence feature screen
(Section~5.3), and that occurrence skill decays with forecast horizon
(Section~5.6, Figure~4). We give both results here as tables, to make them
directly reproducible rather than only visually apparent.

Table~\ref{tab:supp-stability} shows the $15$ most frequently selected
features (of $d=49$; Section~3.3) under $L_1$-logistic stability selection
($200$ subsamples of $60\%$ of the training data, selection frequency = share
of subsamples in which a feature's coefficient is non-zero). The K-index of
convective instability and the most recent index lag (\texttt{lag\_1}) are
selected in essentially every subsample in both cities,
consistent with the main text; day-of-year seasonality, integrated
moisture/vapour-transport fields, the east--west mean-sea-level-pressure
gradient, and the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) index also recur near the top
of both lists.

\section{The dependent-hurdle coupling: a null result}
\label{sec:supp-coupling}

The GP--EVT hurdle of the main paper (Section~4.2) couples its occurrence and
intensity latents through a linear model of coregionalisation, whose off-diagonal
correlation $\rho$ is a \emph{borrowing-strength} parameter ($\rho=0$ recovers the
independent hurdle). The motivation is that, because the excess is observed only on
the few extreme days, letting the abundant occurrence data inform the tail could
stabilise intensity estimation. We test that mechanism directly here and find
that, for this hurdle at this data volume, \emph{coupling is weakly identified and
neither helps nor hurts}; we therefore retain it only as a harmless nested
generalisation of the independent hurdle, not as a source of skill.

\paragraph{Coupling does not change forecast skill.} Re-fitting the
$95$th-percentile bake-off model ($+$ERA5, $h=1$, seed-averaged over three seeds,
$600$ epochs) with the coupling switched off is indistinguishable from the coupled
fit on both proper scores (Table~\ref{tab:supp-couple}): the tail-CRPS and Brier
skills are identical in London and marginally \emph{higher} without coupling in
Paris. Whatever the GP--EVT hurdle achieves (Table~2 of the main paper), it does
not owe to the coregionalisation.

\paragraph{Recovery is weak on synthetic data.} On data simulated from the
generative model with a known occurrence--intensity correlation ($\xi=0.20$; three
seeds each), the coupled hurdle recovers $\rho$ only weakly and noisily ---
$\widehat\rho=0.33\pm0.48$, $0.56\pm0.25$, and $0.79\pm0.05$ at
$\rho_{\mathrm{true}}=0.3$, $0.7$, and $0.9$ respectively --- and the
tail-log-likelihood gain from coupling is negligible at every setting. Crucially,
unlike an over-parameterised base model whose independent intensity head can
collapse (so that coupling appears to rescue it), the single-layer GP hurdle used
here is already stable: there is no instability for borrowing strength to cure, so
the benefit coregionalisation is designed to provide does not materialise.

\paragraph{Real-data estimates.} Fitting the coupled hurdle on the real series
(five seeds) gives $\widehat\rho=+0.55\pm0.16$ in London and $+0.38\pm0.04$ in
Paris. Given the weak identification just documented, these cannot be read as
measurements of real occurrence--intensity dependence; we report them for
completeness and draw no conclusion about the presence or absence of exploitable
coupling.

In short, the dependent-hurdle coupling is a sound nested generalisation of the
independent hurdle but is empirically inert at this data volume: it is retained
because it does no harm and reduces to the independent model as $\rho\to0$, not
because it improves forecasts.
